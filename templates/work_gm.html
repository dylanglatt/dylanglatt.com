GM: 48-Model Delivery

Ipsos MMA

The Problem

General Motors has 48 nameplates, each requiring its own marketing mix model built and maintained separately. Results had to be delivered on four different cadences simultaneously: weekly, monthly, quarterly, and annually. The scale was unlike any other engagement — and there was no existing process that could handle it.

The delivery structure added another layer of complexity. Engineering was done by a team offshore. Client-facing work sat with a small US team. I was part of that US team — which meant sitting in the middle of both relationships at once, responsible for what came in from the offshore team and what went out to GM.

What I Did

My role spanned both ends of the delivery chain. On the engineering side, I worked directly with the offshore engineering team on data collection and model validation — making sure inputs were clean, outputs were correct, and nothing slipped through on any of the 48 models across any given delivery cycle. On the client side, I was part of translating that work into results GM could read and act on.

The volume made ad hoc coordination impossible. To make it work, I helped build the QA processes that gave the US team visibility into what the offshore engineering team was producing — structured validation checks, clear handoff points, and consistent standards across models so issues could be caught early rather than surfacing at delivery.

With four different timeframes running in parallel, the question was never just whether a model was correct — it was whether the right model was ready at the right time. The processes we built made that trackable and repeatable instead of reactive.

The Result

A small US team could reliably monitor and deliver results across all 48 models on every timeframe — at a scale and operational complexity the firm hadn’t handled before. The QA layer meant issues were caught in validation, not in client meetings.
