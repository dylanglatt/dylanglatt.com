Marketing Mix Model Version Control

Ipsos MMA · 2025–2026

The Problem

The marketing mix models I worked with at Ipsos were large, complex Excel files shared across analysts in multiple international offices. There was no version control, no change documentation, and no consistent structure. Formulas were hardcoded throughout. Models were linked to external files that had moved or been renamed. When someone in one time zone made a change, there was no record of what changed, why, or what it might break.

The result: models crashed constantly. Diagnosing a crash meant manually tracing through hundreds of cells with no documentation to lean on. Coordinating a fix across time zones took days. The same problems recurred because there was no structure preventing them.

What I Did

I treated it as a systems problem, not a one-off fix. The goal was a model structure that anyone could follow, update safely, and hand off without creating new problems.

I built a consistent template with a clear separation between inputs, calculations, and outputs. Anyone opening the model could immediately see where data came in, where the logic lived, and where results were read out — no hunting through the file to understand what was connected to what.

I centralized all shared assumptions into a single reference area. Previously, the same assumption might be hardcoded in a dozen different places — meaning a single update required finding and changing every instance, with no guarantee you’d caught them all. Centralizing it meant one change updated everything, and the risk of inconsistency went away.

I removed links to external files and made each model self-contained. Those links were the primary source of crashes — a file moved, a sheet got renamed, and the model broke silently. Removing them made the models stable by default.

I introduced lightweight change logs and consistent naming conventions, then centralized collaboration in dedicated Microsoft Teams channels — one per model — so every change, question, and handoff was visible and stayed attached to the work it referenced. The overhead was minimal; the improvement to coordination was immediate.

The Result

Crash frequency dropped sharply. The back-and-forth across time zones that had been eating days went away — because the models were self-explanatory and changes were documented, analysts could pick up where someone else left off without a handoff call. Output quality improved because the underlying structure was reliable, and updates could be made with confidence instead of caution.
